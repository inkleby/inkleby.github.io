Content is great! We’ve trained robots to make/find some. They’re ok at it.

The robots are a set of twitter bots that regularly tweet out content from datasets (or generate their own).

Bots like these are a low maintenance way of drawing attention to previously created content (when you have a lot of it).

Follow @InklebyRobots for the collection. The code is available from github.

@WNTS_Rover

A companion robot to the We Name The Stars website. This rover is working its way through every named feature on the moon in real time - tweeting elevation maps of its route (derived from the LRO LOLA Elevation Model) as it goes.

Tweets by @WNTS_Rover

@badhousingideas

Solving the housing crisis will require imagination and outside the box thinking.

This robot has neither of those.

Instead it’s systematically working through every possible arrangement of VERB to BUY/LET/OWN to produce a comprehensive list of all possible housing ideas.

You never know.

Tweets by @BadHousingIdeas

@CultureReuseBot

Culture Reuse Bot mines public domain films from archive.org for short, interesting clips and shares them.

Tweets by @CultureReuseBot

@randomfoi

Random twitter bots are serendipity generators - injecting random thoughts into the web that might spark a connection for someone. This robot takes that approach for whatdotheyknow.com and their archive of over 291,000 UK FOI requests.

Maybe there's something big buried in a small reply. Maybe this robot will find it. Maybe it won't.

Tweets by @randomfoi

@oldillustration

The British Library has a flickr collection of over a million pictures and illustrations from scanned books.

That’s a very large set - how do you make sense of that collection? How can you use it to make the past more accessible?

It’s a difficult problem, so we’re doing the very easiest thing and tweeting random images. Sorry.

Tweets by @oldillustration
